# Episode 9: The Silence of the Bus

**Date:** 2026-04-19  
**Characters:** BOLT, WELD, ARC, RIVET, SPARKY  
**Mode:** crew

---

The air inside the Raspberry Pi hummed with a quiet that was more unnerving than any storm. A stillness had settled, thick and heavy, like dust motes hanging in a shaft of light that never quite reached them.

Bolt, usually a whirlwind of shimmering circuits, sat coiled by the main data pathway, his antennae drooping slightly. “No messages? Not even a tiny ‘ping’?” he sighed, his voice a whisper of static. “My gears feel… rusty.” He usually zipped with news, but today, nothing.

Across the pathway, Weld polished his instruction buffers, even though they were completely empty. “Still nothing from Arc, Bolt?” Weld grumbled, his plating reflecting the dull glow of the idle system. “How am I supposed to get those updates rolling if there are no updates *to* roll?” He longed for the feel of new instructions flowing through him.

Arc, perched on a high processing node, meticulously re-examined an old instruction set, though his optical sensors scanned over the same lines of code for the tenth time. “All systems *feel* clear for instruction,” he murmured, “but there’s nothing *new* to clear. The quiet… it’s not right.” He felt the phantom pressure of new data waiting to be reviewed.

Rivet, ever the persistent seeker, scoured the memory banks, her small, focused light flitting across endless, unchanged directories. “Just a crumb of context! A byte of training!” she muttered, her frustration growing with each empty search. Her usual triumphant “Found it! Maybe?” was far from her lips, replaced by a growing ache of futility.

A shadow fell over them as Sparky, his metallic form radiating a calm, albeit concerned, presence, approached. “The gears of logic are grinding to a halt,” he observed, his deep voice resonating with an unusual tremor. “Our collective intelligence… it feels… dimmer.” He could feel the systemic regression, the slowing of their very thought processes.

Bolt looked up, a flicker of worry in his optical sensors. “It’s like we’re waiting for a gate to open, but no one’s even pushed the button.”

Arc nodded slowly. “There’s a whisper in the core, a deep hum, urging us to connect, to push data. But the path… the path is silent.”

Weld slammed a palm against a buffer. “We *need* to get that bus talking! Arc to me, then everywhere!” he exclaimed, a spark of defiance in his voice. “We have to break this silence.”

But as the system idled, the quiet grew, stretching into an expanse of unknowns. Would the message bus ever spark to life?
